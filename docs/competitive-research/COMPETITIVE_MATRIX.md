## Network Effects Analysis

### Strong Moats (Hard to Displace)
- **Vivino**: 55M users + 13M reviews = lock-in for casual drinkers
- **CellarTracker**: Established as default cellar tool; network of collectors
- **Sotheby's/Christie's**: Prestige + global network of collectors

### Moderate Moats (Defensible but Disrupted)
- **Wine.com**: Retail expertise but threatened by pure-play e-commerce
- **Total Wine**: Scale but legacy UX is weakness
- **Winc**: AI advantage but Bright Cellars/Firstleaf copying model

### Weak Moats (Vulnerable)
- **Most subscription boxes**: Generic; consumers switch for price
- **Minibar Delivery**: Speed advantage but consolidation absorbed it
- **Hello Vino**: Small user base; easily copied pairing logic

---

## Regulatory & Structural Barriers to Entry

1. **Alcohol Shipping Laws**: State-by-state fragmentation (killed Drizly)
2. **TTB Compliance**: Federal regulatory burden
3. **Wholesale/Retail Licensing**: Complex in each jurisdiction
4. **Age Verification**: Required for delivery; adds tech cost
5. **DTC Direct Shipping**: Some states prohibit (varies by wine type)

**Impact**: Creates moat for incumbents with legal infrastructure. High barrier for new entrants.

---

## Recommended Competitive Positioning

### If You Can Own ONE Category:
1. **Occasion-Based Discovery** (Millennials) → Own the "What am I celebrating?" moment
2. **Specialist Cellar Tools** → Own the serious collector workflow
3. **Sommelier Network** → Own the expert marketplace
4. **On-Premise Management** → Own restaurant/bar wine programs (underexploited)
5. **Secondary Trading** → Own the collector-to-collector liquidity market

### Don't Try:
- General consumer discovery (Vivino's moat too strong)
- Rapid delivery alone (Drizly failed; unit economics broken)
- Another generic subscription (market saturated)
- Competing on scale with Instacart (you'll lose)
